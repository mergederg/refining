Improving Tickets With Backlog Refining

Better tickets and processes can...

  • Reduce ticket times and turnover
  • Identify and deal with requirements issues earlier
  • Facilitate estimation prior to work
  • Make everyone happier!

What is it?

Refining: Product owner/project management and devs reviewing upcoming work for do-ability and assigning estimates on time or complexity via voting.

Why do it?

Ensure the board/backlog/task list (whatever we want to call the set of things developers pull from in their day to day activities) remains populated with items that are relevant, detailed, actionable and roughly estimated.

Why do it?

The feedback cycle at development is far longer than it can be in refining meetings, so person-hours stand to be saved by shifting those conversations there prior to them ever becoming dev assignments. Remember the graph from last time!

Why do it?

Apart from improving ticket quality, this process also produces estimates (as time or complexity, according to org preferences) out of the process for 'free', doing away with the need for the "how long will this take?" phase many of our tickets go through upon first landing on a dev's board.

Process

  • Anyone can put new tickets in the backlog to be refined for a round of development.
  • Once the list of tickets is complete, a refining meeting allowing for ~3-5 minutes per ticket is scheduled.
  • Developers check this list and take a look at the areas of code, data models and plugins in question to be able to make an informed estimate on it. They also review the proposed tickets for ambiguity and unmet dependencies.

The Refining Meeting

  • Tickets are reviewed and pointed one by one.
    • Reading the ticket: Hopefully people have already done this.
    • Discussion: Developers raise any issues with the ticket or questions they have about the task. If dependencies are unmet (for example, calling for setup ), enablement tickets for those dependencies have to be created and executed first prior to the ticket under discussion being actionable.
    • Pointing/Estimating: Once no questions are outstanding on a ticket, developers vote (planning poker or physical cards) on an estimate. Different strategies exist as to what this number represents and how it is rendered down to one estimate -- some teams take the highest, some take the lowest, others take the mean, median or mode, but the important thing is that its an informed decision made by the development group on a now-refined ticket with as full a transfer of requirements as possible from stakeholders.
